Deep learning (DL), a subfield of machine learning, emphasizes the use of multi-layered neural networks for tasks like classification, regression, and learning representations. Inspired by biological neuroscience, it involves stacking artificial neurons into layers and "training" them to interpret data. The term "deep" refers to the use of multiple layers—anywhere from three to several hundred or even thousands. Learning in these models can be supervised, semi-supervised, or unsupervised.[2]

Common architectures in deep learning include fully connected networks, deep belief networks, recurrent neural networks, convolutional neural networks, generative adversarial networks, transformers, and neural radiance fields. These models have been successfully applied to areas like computer vision, speech recognition, natural language processing, machine translation, bioinformatics, drug discovery, medical imaging, climate science, materials inspection, and game-playing systems—often matching or exceeding human-level performance.[3][4][5]

Early neural networks drew inspiration from how biological systems, especially the human brain, process information and distribute communication. However, modern neural networks are not designed to mimic brain function and are generally considered poor models for that purpose.[6]

Overview
Most contemporary deep learning models rely on multi-layered neural networks such as CNNs and transformers, though they may also incorporate propositional formulas or latent variables organized layer-wise in deep generative models like deep belief networks or deep Boltzmann machines.[7]

At its core, deep learning refers to a class of algorithms that use a hierarchy of layers to transform raw input into increasingly abstract and composite representations. For instance, in an image recognition model, the input might be an image as a pixel tensor. The first layer might detect basic shapes like lines and circles; the second layer could combine edges; the third might recognize a nose and eyes; and the fourth could identify a face.

Crucially, deep learning can automatically determine which features to assign to each level. Before deep learning, machine learning often required manual feature engineering to prepare data for classification. In contrast, deep learning discovers useful feature representations on its own—though some manual tuning (e.g., number and size of layers) remains necessary.[8][2]

The "deep" in deep learning refers to the number of transformation layers. More technically, deep learning systems have a significant credit assignment path (CAP) depth—the chain of transformations from input to output. In feedforward networks, CAP depth equals the number of hidden layers plus one (including the output layer). In recurrent networks, where signals may pass through a layer multiple times, CAP depth can be unlimited.[9] There’s no strict threshold separating shallow from deep learning, but most researchers agree deep learning involves CAP depth greater than two. CAP depth of two is already a universal approximator capable of emulating any function.[10] Additional layers don’t increase that theoretical capability but help extract better features than shallow models, making learning more effective.
