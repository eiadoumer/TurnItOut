Later on Wednesday, the U.S. sided with Israel. President Donald Trump described the violence in Lebanon as “a separate skirmish,” even though Hezbollah had joined the war in support of Iran.

In Israel, Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has faced intense political pressure since the U.S. and Iran signed a ceasefire that Israel had little or no active role in shaping. None of Israel’s war goals—which Netanyahu had presented to his people as part of an existential struggle with Iran—had been achieved, disappointing war supporters.

Moreover, under the truce terms published yesterday, a 10-point peace plan proposed by Iran has been accepted as the basis for negotiations set to begin this weekend in Islamabad. Early details of the Iranian plan suggest Iran would keep its nuclear stockpile and could gain financially from shipping levies through the Strait of Hormuz, as well as from tariffs and sanctions relief promised by U.S. President Donald Trump on his Truth Social account.

This is far from the earlier 15-point U.S. list of demands, which included fully reopening the strait without conditions, Iran giving up its enriched uranium, ending its ballistic missile program, and halting support for proxy groups like Yemen’s Houthis, Lebanon’s Hezbollah, and various armed groups in Iraq.

Arguing that Lebanon is exempt from the ceasefire, Israel launched its heaviest bombardment on its neighbor in recent months on Wednesday. In about ten minutes, the Israeli military carried out over 100 strikes on what it said were Hezbollah targets, hitting Beirut, southern Lebanon, and the eastern Bekaa Valley. At least 254 people were killed, 91 of them in Beirut alone.

The attacks have drawn condemnation from many nations and international organizations, including Spain, France, the UK, the UN, and Pakistan—which brokered the ceasefire and explicitly stated that Lebanon was covered by it.

In response, Iranian state media announced that Tehran is now considering withdrawing from the truce and has already said it will reimpose restrictions on the strategically vital Strait of Hormuz.

Israel, for its part, says it is not trying to kill the ceasefire by striking Lebanon. Charles Freilich, a former Israeli deputy national security adviser, told Al Jazeera that the strikes were motivated solely by an “opportunity to hit numerous mid to high-level Hezbollah fighters,” not to undermine the ceasefire, which both the U.S. and Israel maintain does not include Lebanon.
