 convolutional neural network (CNN) is a type of feedforward deep learning network that learns features by optimizing filters (or kernels). CNNs are widely used to process and predict from various data types, including text, images, and audio.[1] They have long been the standard for deep learning in computer vision[2] and image processing, though they’ve recently been challenged in some applications by newer architectures like transformers.

CNNs avoid the vanishing and exploding gradient problems seen in earlier networks during backpropagation, thanks to regularization from shared weights over fewer connections.[3][4] For example, a fully connected layer processing a 100×100 pixel image would need 10,000 weights per neuron. But with cascaded convolution (or cross-correlation) kernels,[5][6] each convolutional layer requires only 25 weights for 5×5 tiles.[7][8] Higher-layer features are extracted from broader context windows than lower-layer features.

Applications of CNNs include:

image and video recognition,[9]

recommender systems,[10]

image classification,

image segmentation,

medical image analysis,

natural language processing,[11]

brain–computer interfaces,[12] and

financial time series.[13]

CNNs are also called shift-invariant or space-invariant artificial neural networks due to their shared-weight architecture, where convolution kernels slide over input features and produce translation-equivariant responses known as feature maps.[14][15] However, most CNNs are not truly translation-invariant because of downsampling operations.[16]

Feedforward neural networks are typically fully connected, meaning every neuron in one layer connects to all neurons in the next. This full connectivity makes them prone to overfitting. Common regularization methods include penalizing parameters during training (e.g., weight decay) or reducing connectivity (skipped connections, dropout, etc.). Large, robust datasets also help CNNs learn general principles rather than dataset-specific biases.[17]

CNNs were inspired by biological processes[18][19][20][21]—specifically, the connectivity pattern in the animal visual cortex, where individual cortical neurons respond only to a restricted visual field region called the receptive field. These receptive fields overlap to cover the entire field.

Compared to other image classification algorithms, CNNs require relatively little preprocessing. The network learns to optimize filters through automated learning, whereas traditional algorithms rely on hand-engineered filters. This simplifies and automates the process, improving efficiency and scalability while reducing human bottlenecks.
